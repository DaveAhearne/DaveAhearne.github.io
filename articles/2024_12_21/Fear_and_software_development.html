The fear of percieved failure, software development & the tutorial trap.

Teaching children to draw seems to be much easier than teaching adults to draw.

If you give a child a set of crayons or paintbrushes they will usually have already enthusiastically picked them up and started to decorate the canvas (or anything else nearby) before you can even begin to explain that they have the freedom to draw literally anything that they like.

But adults? Adults are different.

There is even a name for the anxiety that comes from this. "Blank Page Syndrome". But why don't children have this?

The Fear of Failure

The reason that children have this approach is that they have not yet been burned by failure. The hand that touches the hot stove will not touch it again, but the hand that has never touched the stove is usually pretty enthusiastic about the whole thing...

But adults know it well, they know how much it hurts because of experiences like:

  • Working really hard on something, only for it to be mocked. "What is this, did you even try?"
  • Seeing someone else be an expert at a particular skill or hobby, and feeling devastated as a beginner because you can't do the most "simple" things
  • c

The problem is that while the strategies that help us avoid physical pain (and a burned hand) inhibit our ability to learn and grow. learning is painful and uncomfortable. It is the process of taking what was familiar, and struggling with a new concept, discarding what was familiar and contending with the unknown.

Retreat to Process and Familiarity

Most people will retreated to one of two defence mechanisms when this happens,

avoidance or process, they will avoid the problem by procrastinating "I'm not in the mood right now, I want to do X/Y/Z"

Material Hoarding and the Tutorial Trap

People will do this thing where they'll say, oh I can't do X yet - i'll look for a tutorial, so they find a tutorial they'll buy it. Some motivated people will start progress on the tutorial if they're truly interested but then the tutorial ends. Then the fear creeps in...

The problem is that these people are not comfortable attempting a problem without having their hand held, so they are always looking for the next source of hand holding to jump to. However, there is good news.

Building software isn't like constructing a building. If you're constructing a building the foundations are critical, anything built upon weak foundations is doomed to failure, no matter how nice your brickwork is, no matter how much time and money you spent picking out those curtains, it must come down.

This means that before you make any decision you have to be sure it's the right one, because your mistakes multiply and there is a real risk of time and money being lost.

Where do you go from here?

Software however, is not like construction at all, (see the history of the waterfall methodology). It is not only possible to completely re-do the foundations of a software project halfway through construction, sometimes it is even necessary!

“We don't make mistakes, just happy little accidents.”
— Bob Ross

Think instead of your software as a thick oil painting, a beautiful canvas where experiments have been performed, some successful, some not and painted over hundreds and hundreds of times.

Become comfortable being uncomfortable
